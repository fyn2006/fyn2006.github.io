Geotechnical Engineering Research Co-op

Currently completing a research assistant co-op at the University of Victoria's Geotechnical Engineering Laboratory. The research focuses on how freeze-thaw cycling affects the structural integrity of aggregate and subgrade soils used in transportation infrastructure.

The position involves assisting graduate students with research tasks, including preparing, testing, and analyzing soil samples under controlled environmental conditions using both small-scale and large-scale testing methods.

This research will then be used to write a paper to be submitted to a geotechnical engineering conference.

TESTING PROCESS

Testing Process

Both the large-scale and small-scale test boxes follow generally the same testing procedure with slight alterations.

The general testing process consisted of:

  1. Measuring and mixing the subgrade and aggregate soils
  2. Filling the test box and compacting each layer to its specified height, placing sensors every few layers
  3. Running the soil structure through repeated freeze-thaw cycles
  4. Performing structural analysis tests on the soil structure
  5. Removing the top layer and analyzing deformation of the subgrade
  6. Removing damaged subgrade and rebuilding the structure to repeat the test under different conditions
LARGE BOX TESTING

Large-Scale Test Box

The large-scale test box was used to model roadway material behavior at a larger physical scale.

Sensors were placed throughout the structure during construction so changes in the soil temperature, moisture content, pressure, and surface structure could be monitored throughout the test.

SMALL BOX TESTING

Small-Scale Test Box

The small-scale test box was used to perform controlled and repeatable freeze-thaw experiments with a smaller amount of material and faster sample preparation.

The smaller box allows multiple material configurations to be tested efficiently while maintaining consistent compaction, moisture content, and environmental exposure conditions between experiments.
