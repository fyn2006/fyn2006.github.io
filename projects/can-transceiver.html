CAN Transceiver PCB

The objective of this project was to design and manufacture a PCB that allows UART-capable devices to communicate over a CAN network. The constraints of this project were the budget and the location. The project had to be under 50$ and located in Nelson, where I was working at the time. I had no access to real lab equipment and only three boards' worth of parts. This project consisted of five main stages: 1. Creating a schematic based on a reference design supplied by a mentor 2. Hardboard prototyping the circuit to verify functionality and validate component connections before PCB development 3. Designing the PCB layout with consideration for manufacturability, connector placement, routing quality, and signal integrity 4. Manufacturing the board through PCBWay and assembling the completed hardware 5. Testing and integrating the finished PCB for use within the AUVIC autonomous underwater vehicle platform

Schematic Design

The schematic was designed in KiCad based on a reference circuit supplied by a mentor. A project constraint was that only a limited set of components was available, requiring the design to be built around the provided parts.

Figure 1 — CAN transceiver schematic

Schematic Prototyping

To prototype the PCB, the components were soldered onto hardboard and tested using a setup consisting of two Arduinos.

Figure 2 — Hardboard prototype

PCB Design

The PCB layout was created in KiCad. The process involved selecting component footprints, creating the board layout, and routing the traces. The completed design was then sent to PCBWay for manufacture.

Figure 3 — PCB layout design

Manufactured Board

The final board was manufactured with the help of PCBWay and produced as a physical PCB for assembly and testing. The board has yet to be assembled due to a lack of parts.
