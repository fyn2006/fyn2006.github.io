Picnic Table Motor Boat

Designed and built a motorized floating picnic table to enjoy a picnic out on the water.

This project is currently on its second iteration, with plans to build a third in the summer of 2026.

This project consisted of four major stages:

  1. Developing the initial picnic table boat concept
  2. Constructing the first-generation prototype
  3. Identifying limitations
  4. Designing and building the improved second-generation model
YEAR 1 — VERSION ONE

First Prototype

The first design focused on creating a floating picnic table with some form of automatic propulsion.

Objectives & Constraints

The objective of the first design was to create a floating picnic table that could be propelled by a motor and carry enough people for a picnic.

The primary constraints of the project were budget and time. The total project cost was limited to approximately $100 CAD, and the entire design and construction process had to be completed in a week.

Figure 1 — Version 1
DESIGN REVIEW

Problems Identified

Issues Found

The original frame experienced flexing under load, the steering system lacked responsiveness, and the propulsion mounting introduced excessive vibration during operation.

Weight distribution also reduced overall stability when passengers shifted positions.

YEAR 2 — VERSION TWO

Redesigned Model

The second-generation model was redesigned to solve the major issues identified during the operation of the original prototype.

Objectives & Constraints

The primary objective of the redesign was to improve structural rigidity, passenger stability, steering performance, and propulsion reliability without significantly increasing overall weight.

Constraints included reusing components where possible, maintaining portability, and improving durability while preserving passenger space.

Figure 2 — Redesigned second-generation model
SECOND GENERATION IMPROVEMENTS

Problems Solved

Several major improvements were introduced into the second-generation design to improve reliability and overall handling.

Implemented Improvements

The redesigned model included a reinforced frame, improved weight distribution, stronger propulsion mounting, and revised steering geometry to reduce vibration and improve maneuverability.

Additional reinforcement also improved passenger stability and reduced structural flex during operation.

FUTURE DEVELOPMENT

Future Changes

Future revisions could focus on improving efficiency, reducing overall weight, and refining the propulsion and steering systems.

Potential Improvements

Possible future changes include lighter materials, improved flotation geometry, integrated electrical systems, cleaner cable routing, and a more refined propulsion mounting system.

Additional redesigns may also improve transportability, durability, and long-term operation in varying water conditions.
